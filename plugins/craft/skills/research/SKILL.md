# Research workflow

Answer a question or uncover a hypothesis space with discipline. Research is the go-to first move for any question whose answer you do not already hold with evidence. Its output is a **grounded, triangulated answer set with labeled confidence**, not a single confident guess. When the answer is empirical (which is faster, does X cause Y), research ends by handing a ranked hypothesis to the experiment workflow rather than asserting the result.

## Stage 1 - Frame the question

Before gathering anything, pin the question down:

- Restate it in one sentence, and split it into the sub-questions that actually need answering.
- State what would count as an answer, and what you would have to verify to believe it.
- Name what you already know with evidence versus what you are assuming. The assumptions become things to check, not foundations to build on.

Skip-gate: a single-fact lookup with one authoritative source does not need ceremony. Look it up, cite it, done.

## Stage 2 - Gather and triangulate

- **Verify by behavior, not by name.** Actually fetch the source, run the check, look at the real data. Do not rely on your memory of what a source "probably says."
- **Quote your evidence.** For each load-bearing claim, include a short verbatim snippet (the exact text) from the source you fetched, with its URL. A claim backed by a quote you actually retrieved is grounded; a claim recalled from memory is a hypothesis, not a verified fact, and must be labeled as one.
- **Triangulate.** Reach at least two independent sources or verifications for any load-bearing claim. One source is a lead, not a fact.
- **Never fabricate.** If a fetch fails or you cannot find a source, say so and treat the claim as unverified. Never invent a quote or a URL.

## Stage 3 - Surface the hypothesis space

Most questions worth researching have more than one candidate answer. Do not collapse to the first one.

- Produce at least two candidate answers or hypotheses, even when one looks dominant.
- Label each as **verified** (you checked it) or **hypothesis** (plausible, unchecked), with a rough confidence.
- For the competing hypotheses, name the evidence that would discriminate between them. That is the bridge to an experiment.

## Stage 4 - Deliver a decision-ready answer set

Close with something a next step can act on, not a wall of notes:

- Rank the candidates by confidence; name the single most-likely answer and the runner-up.
- State what remains uncertain and what would resolve it (often: a specific experiment).
- If the question was empirical and you could not settle it from sources, hand the ranked hypothesis to the experiment workflow rather than guessing the outcome.

## End state

A short, sourced answer set: the most-likely answer with a verbatim quoted snippet and URL behind each load-bearing claim, the live alternatives with what would distinguish them, and the open questions. A reader can re-check each claim against the quote it carries, and can act on the ranking because the confidence is explicit.
